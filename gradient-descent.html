Gradient Descent Algorithms

Description, Explanation, Illustration.

Posted by Louise on April 24, 2017
For this first post, I am hoping to complete some excellent blog posts from Sebastian Ruder and Till Bergman on gradient descent, since it is the most common algorithm for optimization problems, especially in Machine Learning.

We'll go through the generic Gradient Descent algorithm, and then explain and illustrate possible optimizations, in order to speed-up the convergence, especially in the scope of very big data sets.

Let us first set some vocabulary and notations. Gradient descent is a method to find the minimizer of an objective function , usually noted: $$ \begin{align*} J & : & \mathbb{R}^d \longrightarrow & \mathbb{R}^n \\ & & \theta \longmapsto & J(\theta) \end{align*} $$ Where \( \theta \) is the vector of the model's parameters. This minimizer is found by updating the parameters \( \theta \) in the opposite direction of the gradient at \( \theta \), i.e. \( \nabla J(\theta)\), controlled by a learning rate \( \eta \) that gives the size of the steps during the descent.

Here is a little animation to illustrate the principle. 

import numpy as np
# From calculation, it is expected that the local minimum occurs at x=9/4

x_old = 0 # The value does not matter as long as abs(x_new - x_old) > precision
x_new = 6 # The algorithm starts at x=6
gamma = 0.01 # step size
precision = 0.00001

def df(x):
    y = 4 * x**3 - 9 * x**2
    return y

while abs(x_new - x_old) > precision:
    x_old = x_new
    x_new += -gamma * df(x_old)

print("The local minimum occurs at %f" % x_new)

Gradient descent is a first-order iterative optimization algorithm.

  • First order means that the first derivative of the function to be minimized is involved.
  • Iterative means each step is supposed to make an improvement.
